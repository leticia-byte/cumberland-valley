The Stone That Stayed

The clinic burned to the ground in 1996. The stone sign holder out front was the only thing left standing — and it became the logo. This direction makes that the whole site: what endures when everything around it gets sold, consolidated, or rebuilt.

The surprise is Hearth. The logo is navy and gray — cold, correct, a little institutional. Barn clay and hearth amber are what the logo can't say on its own: this is a farm practice, and there's a fire in it. Used once per screen, never twice.

Typography

Headings — Fraunces 600
Hagerstown's veterinarian, keeping care close to home.

Fraunces has weight and a little wobble in the serifs — it reads carved, not printed. It's the type equivalent of a hand-lettered sign set into stone. Not corporate, not cutesy.

Body — Inter 400 / 17px / 1.7

Dr. Jen Dolan has worked at Cumberland Valley Veterinary Clinic for over thirty years. In [YEAR], she bought the practice, and from that day forward she committed to keeping it independent. Today, Cumberland Valley is one of the last few independent veterinary practices in Washington County.

Inter at 17px is the accessibility floor for this audience — large, high-contrast, boring in the best way. Body never drops below 17px anywhere on the site.

Rationale

This direction answers the thesis's core tension — independent vs. corporate — with permanence instead of argument. It never says "we're not a chain." It shows a stone that outlasted a fire and a woman who's been here thirty years. For a blue-collar, farming, older clientele, that reads as true rather than sold. The restraint on motion is deliberate: this audience will not tolerate a site that moves more than it has to.
